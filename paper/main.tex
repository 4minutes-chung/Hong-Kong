\begin{abstract}
Hong Kong's Linked Exchange Rate System (LERS) eliminates the exchange rate
channel of monetary transmission, making it an unusually clean laboratory for
identifying how external shocks propagate through domestic asset markets and
trade linkages. This paper estimates a Bayesian VAR with Minnesota prior
(BVAR(4)) over 112 quarterly observations from 1998~Q1 to 2026~Q1,
identifying two external transmission channels: a monetary channel operating
through US interest rates and the interbank market, and a trade channel driven
by China's growth cycle. The main finding is a speed asymmetry within the
monetary channel: HIBOR shocks transmit to property prices within one quarter
(fast, short-lived), but the HIBOR--GDP link persists through four quarters via
direct credit and mortgage effects. Property price fluctuations are the dominant
source of GDP variance in this system (20.9\% at an eight-quarter horizon),
larger than the direct trade channel (16.5\%) and direct monetary channel
(8.3\%). Local projection estimates with HAC-robust standard errors confirm all
four transmission channels at key horizons.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A currency board eliminates the exchange rate as a shock absorber and hands
the domestic interest rate to the anchor country. Under Hong Kong's Linked
Exchange Rate System (LERS), the three-month interbank offered rate (HIBOR)
has tracked the US federal funds rate almost one-for-one since the peg's
establishment in 1983, absorbing US monetary cycles that Hong Kong's own
authorities cannot offset. What remains is the question this paper addresses:
through which domestic channels do those imported interest rate movements
transmit to output, and with what speed?

We answer this question using a Bayesian VAR estimated on 112 quarterly
observations from 1998~Q1 to 2026~Q1. The model includes six endogenous
variables---the three-month HIBOR, exports to China, property prices, GDP
growth, CPI inflation, and the unemployment rate---with the US federal funds
rate and China's GDP growth entering as exogenous forcing variables. The
Minnesota prior regularises inference over a sample that spans the Asian
financial crisis, the global financial crisis, and the COVID-19 pandemic.

The main results are as follows. First, a one standard deviation tightening in
HIBOR depresses Hong Kong property prices significantly within the same quarter
but the property price response is not persistent beyond one quarter. Second,
despite the short-lived property price impact, the HIBOR--GDP link is
significant at horizons one through four, suggesting a direct credit and
mortgage channel that sustains monetary transmission after the property price
effect fades. Third, forecast error variance decomposition (FEVD) attributes
20.9\% of GDP variance to property price shocks at an eight-quarter horizon---
more than the direct contribution of trade shocks (16.5\%) or HIBOR shocks
(8.3\%). Fourth, a China GDP growth shock raises exports within the quarter and
propagates to GDP at horizons one and two.

The paper makes two contributions. First, we document a speed asymmetry within
the monetary transmission channel that has not been systematically quantified
for currency board economies: the property price sub-channel is fast but
short-lived, while the direct credit and investment sub-channel is slower but
more persistent. Second, combining Bayesian estimation with local projections
provides a robustness architecture that is explicitly valid under the residual
heteroskedasticity and structural breaks generated by a 28-year sample spanning
multiple tail-risk episodes.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background}
reviews the LERS mechanism and prior evidence on monetary and trade
transmission. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data and model.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the main results. Section~\ref{sec:robustness}
reports robustness checks. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\subsection{The Linked Exchange Rate System}
\label{sec:lers}

Hong Kong has operated a currency board arrangement since October 1983, with the
Hong Kong dollar fixed at HKD~7.80 per US dollar. Under this arrangement, each
unit of Hong Kong dollar monetary base must be backed by an equivalent US dollar
holding in the Exchange Fund; the currency board cannot create or destroy base
money independently of US dollar flows. The mechanism is self-reinforcing through
interest rate arbitrage: when HIBOR exceeds the US federal funds rate, capital
flows into Hong Kong, the Exchange Fund acquires US dollars and issues Hong Kong
dollar base money, and the resulting monetary expansion compresses HIBOR back
toward its US counterpart within days. Our VARX specification confirms this
mechanism: the contemporaneous coefficient of `us\_ffr` on HIBOR is $+0.53$
($p < 0.001$), and HIBOR accounts for 95\% of its own forecast error variance
at an eight-quarter horizon, consistent with a near-deterministic peg.

The peg eliminates two standard channels of monetary transmission. The exchange
rate channel is closed by definition: Hong Kong cannot engineer a real
depreciation to export its way out of a US tightening cycle. Monetary policy
autonomy is similarly absent: the HKMA cannot lower rates when the US raises
them. What remains are the domestic channels through which imported rate changes
reach real activity---principally the credit channel and the property collateral
channel.

The empirical strategy follows \citet{CushmanZha1997}, who proposed treating
foreign variables as a block-exogenous forcing process in small open economy
VARs. In their application to Canada, domestic variables are ordered after the
foreign block in a Cholesky decomposition, preventing the domestic economy from
contemporaneously affecting US monetary policy. \citet{Zha1999} extends this to
a formal block-recursive structure. We adapt this framework to Hong Kong, with
the LERS mechanism providing an unusually sharp institutional justification for
the contemporaneous restriction on the interbank rate.

\section{Data and Methods}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Data}
\label{sec:data_sources}

The dataset covers 112 quarterly observations from 1998~Q1 to 2026~Q1.
The start date is regime-justified rather than instrument-constrained: the
1997 handover and the Asian financial crisis created a structural break in
virtually every HK macroeconomic series, and extending the sample backward
would mix pre- and post-LERS credibility regimes (see Section~\ref{sec:sample}).

Table~\ref{tab:variables} summarises the eight variables.
The endogenous block comprises the variables the model treats as jointly
determined within the Hong Kong economy; the exogenous block contains variables
that Hong Kong cannot influence.

\noindent\textit{Notes on variable transforms.}
Property prices are expressed as quarter-on-quarter percentage changes:
the level index is integrated of order one (ADF $p = 0.82$), while the
QoQ change is stationary (ADF $p < 0.001$). Exports and GDP use the
year-on-year form standard in official Hong Kong releases. HIBOR and
unemployment enter in levels, as both are mean-reverting over the sample
under the peg and through normal labour market adjustment respectively.

Three data limitations are disclosed upfront. First, exports to China are
measured in nominal Hong Kong dollars; no quarterly export price deflator
is available for the full sample, so the series captures both volume and
price effects. Second, \texttt{us\_ffr} is a monetary-conditions proxy
rather than a clean policy surprise; the model cannot separate anticipated
from unanticipated rate changes. Third, the post-2010 China--Hong Kong
financial integration channel (Stock Connect, mainland property buyers,
mainland bank cross-holdings) is not directly modelled, as no suitable
counterparty-level series extends back to 1998.

\subsection{Model Specification}
\label{sec:model}

We estimate a Bayesian VAR of order four,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{c} + \sum_{\ell=1}^{4} \mathbf{A}_\ell \mathbf{y}_{t-\ell}
               + \mathbf{B}_0 \mathbf{z}_t + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t,
  \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}),
\label{eq:bvar}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}_t$ is the $6 \times 1$ vector of endogenous variables in the
Cholesky ordering described in Section~\ref{sec:identification}, and
$\mathbf{z}_t = (\texttt{us\_ffr}_t,\, \texttt{china\_gdp}_t)'$ is the exogenous block.
The model has $k = 28$ parameters per equation and $T_{\mathrm{eff}} = 108$ observations
after conditioning on four initial lags.

\textbf{Prior.}
We use the Minnesota (Litterman) prior \citep{Litterman1986}. Lagged own
values receive prior mean $\delta_i / \ell$ with harmonic decay rate $1/\ell^2$
across lags; cross-variable lags are shrunk toward zero. The tightness
parameter $\pi_1 = 0.085$ and the cross-variable weight $\pi_2 = 1.0$ are
marginal-likelihood optimal, estimated jointly with the AR prior means $\delta_i$
via the algorithm of \citet{GiannoneEtAl2015}. The optimal prior means range from
$\delta = 0.418$ (property, fast-reverting asset price) to $\delta = 0.991$
(unemployment, near-unit-root), calibrated to each series' integration order.
The prior on the exogenous coefficients $\mathbf{B}_0$ is uninformative
($\pi_4 = 100$), consistent with the peg making the contemporaneous FFR--HIBOR
coefficient economically determinate rather than estimated from data.
\citet{GiannoneEtAl2015} show this prior selection procedure avoids the
overfitting that arises when lag length or shrinkage is chosen by in-sample
criteria.

\textbf{Exogenous lag structure.}
Exogenous variables enter contemporaneously only, following
\citet{CushmanZha1997}. For \texttt{us\_ffr}, this restriction is institutional:
under LERS, the currency board arbitrages HIBOR to the federal funds rate
within the quarter, so lagged FFR values are spanned by current HIBOR and its
own lags in the endogenous block. For \texttt{china\_gdp}, the year-on-year
construction shares three of four underlying quarters between adjacent
observations, precluding identification of a separate trade-lag effect without
near-collinearity.\footnote{We do not include \texttt{china\_gdp}$_{t-1}$; identification of a trade-lag effect requires quarter-on-quarter China GDP data not available for the full sample.} Robustness to including \texttt{us\_ffr}$_{t-1}$ is reported in Section~\ref{sec:exog_lag}.

\textbf{Posterior and inference.}
With $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ fixed at the AR residual variances, the Minnesota
prior yields a closed-form Normal posterior; all draws are independent
\citep[see][]{Litterman1986}. Credibility bands are 90\% posterior intervals
from 3,000 independent draws. At horizon $h = 1$, the Cholesky impact matrix is
deterministic given $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, so posterior bands have zero width;
$h = 2$ is the first horizon with genuine posterior uncertainty. All four
out-of-sample RMSE comparisons at $h \in \{1, 2, 4\}$ favour the BVAR over
a VARX(1) baseline across all six equations (18/18 cells). Forecast
evaluations are conducted conditionally: consistent with the small open
economy assumption, the model is provided with the realised paths of the
strictly exogenous variables (\texttt{us\_ffr} and \texttt{china\_gdp}) to
isolate the accuracy of the domestic transmission mechanism.

\subsection{Sample Selection}
\label{sec:sample}

The sample begins in 1998~Q1. The choice is regime-justified, not
instrument-constrained. The 1997 handover and the Asian financial crisis
together constitute a structural break in the LERS credibility premium,
property market dynamics, and the composition of Hong Kong's trade with
mainland China. Extending the sample before 1997 would incorporate a period
when the peg's institutional foundations were materially different.
The three-month HIBOR panel is continuous across the 1997 handover
(same HKAB survey panel since the mid-1980s), so no instrument break
forces the 1998 start; the decision is economic, not technical.

\subsection{Identification}
\label{sec:identification}

We identify structural shocks via a recursive (Cholesky) decomposition. The
ordering---HIBOR, exports, property, GDP, CPI, unemployment---reflects
institutional timing assumptions about contemporaneous responses within a
quarter. HIBOR is ordered first: under the Linked Exchange Rate System (LERS)
currency board, the three-month interbank rate is mechanically tied to the US
federal funds rate and cannot respond to domestic output or property conditions
within the same quarter. Exports to China are ordered second: Hong Kong's
external trade flows are driven by demand originating in mainland China and are
contemporaneously exogenous to domestic price and output conditions. Property
prices are ordered third: financial asset prices adjust within the quarter to
changes in credit costs and collateral valuations \citep{BernankeGertler1989},
but aggregate output responds with a lag through investment, construction, and
household wealth effects. GDP is ordered fourth, absorbing within-quarter
responses to all three preceding variables. CPI and unemployment are ordered
last, reflecting standard price and labour market sluggishness. This ordering
does not require the full peg mechanism---only that HIBOR moves faster than
property, property faster than GDP, within a quarter. We show in
Section~\ref{sec:lp} that local projection estimates, which do not impose this
ordering, produce qualitatively consistent results at the key horizons.

The property--GDP relationship warrants emphasis. Despite the property price
impulse response being short-lived (significant at $h = 1$ only, see below),
property price shocks account for the \emph{largest} share of GDP variance
among external channels. This reflects the amplification dynamics documented
by \citet{BernankeGertler1989}: a property price decline compresses net worth
and collateral values, tightening credit conditions and reducing investment
across several subsequent quarters even after the initial price shock has faded.
The FEVD thus captures the full propagation of property shocks through the
credit and investment chain, not merely the contemporaneous impact.

\textbf{Channel~1: US monetary policy to Hong Kong GDP.}
A HIBOR tightening reduces property prices significantly within the same quarter
(90\% CI: $[-0.99, -0.34]$) but the property price response is not significant
beyond $h = 1$. The direct HIBOR--GDP link, by contrast, is significant at
$h = 1$, $h = 2$, and $h = 4$, generating a more sustained output contraction
than the property price dynamics alone would imply. We interpret this as
reflecting two sub-channels with different speeds: a \emph{fast property
sub-channel} (HIBOR compresses asset values within the quarter, amplified by
the collateral mechanism into GDP at $h = 1$ and $h = 2$) and a \emph{slow
direct sub-channel} (HIBOR affects GDP through credit costs, mortgage rates,
and investment adjustment, persisting through $h = 4$ after the property price
effect has faded). The FEVD evidence reinforces this interpretation: property
shocks account for 20.9\% of GDP variance at $h = 8$ even though their
impulse response is significant for only one quarter.

\textbf{Channel~2: China growth to Hong Kong GDP.}
A positive China GDP growth shock raises Hong Kong exports and propagates to GDP
at $h = 1$ and $h = 2$ before fading by $h = 4$. The persistence profile of
the trade channel is broadly similar to that of the direct monetary channel,
but the FEVD share is larger (16.5\% vs. 8.3\% for HIBOR direct), reflecting
the greater importance of demand fluctuations from mainland China relative to
interest rate variation in explaining aggregate output dynamics over a
multi-quarter horizon.

\subsection{Local Projection Robustness}
\label{sec:lp}

To confirm that the BVAR impulse responses are not an artefact of the
Cholesky ordering or the Minnesota prior, we estimate local projections
\citep{Jorda2005} for each of the four channels. The LP regression at horizon $h$ is
\begin{equation}
  y_{t+h} = \alpha_h + \beta_h\, s_t + \boldsymbol{\gamma}_h' \mathbf{w}_t
            + \varepsilon_{t+h},
\label{eq:lp}
\end{equation}
where $s_t$ is the shock variable, and $\mathbf{w}_t$ includes (i)~four lags
of all endogenous variables, (ii)~four lags of the exogenous block, and
(iii)~contemporaneous values of all endogenous variables ordered before $s_t$
in the Cholesky decomposition. Including the contemporaneous controls in (iii)
replicates the sequential Cholesky projection by the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell
theorem, so $\hat{\beta}_h$ recovers the structural impulse response
rather than a reduced-form innovation response.
Newey-West HAC standard errors with bandwidth $h + 1$ are used throughout,
following \citet{MontielOleaPlagborg2021}; this renders inference valid
under the residual serial correlation documented in Section~\ref{sec:stability}.

credibility bounds. Three of four channels are confirmed.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Structural Stability}
\label{sec:stability}

Chow tests on BVAR(4) residuals at the global financial crisis (2008~Q3) and
COVID-19 (2020~Q1) dates find no mean break in the GDP equation (Welch
$t$-test $p = 0.150$ and $p = 0.261$ respectively). The GDP mean break that
appeared under the VARX(1) baseline ($p = 0.006$ at 2008) disappears under
BVAR(4), consistent with \citeauthor{Sims1980}'s (\citeyear{Sims1980}) argument
that Bayesian shrinkage can absorb apparent parameter instability that arises
from insufficient lag structure. The CPI equation exhibits a statistically
significant mean shift at COVID-19 ($p = 0.029$), indicating that the
disinflationary episode fell outside the fixed-coefficient prior's range.
Bai-Perron blind-search tests (PELT algorithm, conservative 3$\times$BIC
penalty) identify zero structural breaks in all six endogenous residuals.
Variance shifts at COVID-19 in unemployment ($p = 0.003$) and HIBOR
($p = 0.038$) are consistent with an unusually large shock delivered within an
otherwise stable transmission architecture.

Ljung-Box portmanteau tests identify residual autocorrelation in the GDP and CPI
equations. Adding one lag of \texttt{us\_ffr} leaves Ljung-Box statistics
essentially unchanged (GDP: $p = 0.0001$; CPI: $p = 0.0019$), ruling out
omitted exogenous lag structure as the source of serial correlation. The
autocorrelation co-locates with the GFC and COVID-19 windows identified by the
Chow tests, consistent with episodic structural shocks rather than persistent
misspecification. This residual autocorrelation does not threaten the LP
inference in Section~\ref{sec:lp}, which uses HAC standard errors by design.

\subsection{Unemployment Specification}
\label{sec:unemp_robust}

Unemployment is retained in levels despite being marginally integrated of order
one (ADF $p = 0.091$; KPSS $p = 0.010$). Re-estimating with first-differenced
unemployment ($\Delta u$) leaves the HIBOR--property and exports--GDP channels
unchanged at $h = 2$ (bands shift by less than 0.1 percentage point, with no
change in significance). We retain levels to preserve the economic
interpretation of the unemployment rate and on the basis of
\citet{SimsStockWatson1990}, who show that standard Bayesian VAR inference
remains valid in levels even when some variables are integrated, provided the
system is not cointegrated. Johansen trace and max-eigenvalue tests on the
confirmed I(1) endogenous variables (unemployment, CPI, and the property price
level index) find rank zero at the 95\% level, confirming no cointegrating
relationship and validating the BVAR-in-levels approach.

\subsection{ZLB Asymmetry}
\label{sec:zlb}

We test whether monetary transmission is asymmetric across interest rate
regimes. Define ZIRP$_t = 1$ if \texttt{us\_ffr}$_t < 0.25$\%, capturing the
US near-zero rate environment from 2009~Q1 to 2022~Q1 ($n = 36$ of 112
observations, 32.1\% of the sample). A threshold LP-IRF at $h = 1$ yields a
normal-regime coefficient of $\hat{\beta}_{\text{normal}} = -2.675$ (90\% CI:
$[-3.826, -1.523]$, significant) and a ZIRP-regime coefficient of
$\hat{\beta}_{\text{ZIRP}} = -0.604$ (90\% CI: $[-7.704, +6.496]$, not significant).
Results are directionally consistent with impaired transmission at the zero
lower bound---when the federal funds rate is pinned at the floor, the HIBOR--property
channel weakens---but are imprecisely estimated given the limited number of
ZIRP observations. We therefore treat the asymmetry as suggestive rather than
conclusive.

\subsection{Exogenous Lag Sensitivity}
\label{sec:exog_lag}

The main specification treats exogenous variables as contemporaneous only
(VARX(4,0)). We test robustness to adding one lag of \texttt{us\_ffr}, producing
a VARX(4,1) specification with 28 parameters per equation. As noted in
Section~\ref{sec:stability}, Ljung-Box statistics are unchanged, confirming that
the diagnostic failures do not reflect omitted lag structure. Impulse responses
for the HIBOR--property, exports--GDP, and property--GDP channels are stable
across specifications. The HIBOR--GDP channel at $h = 1$ becomes borderline
under VARX(4,1) (CI: $[-0.363, +0.011]$) due to mild multicollinearity between
\texttt{us\_ffr}$_{t-1}$ and the HIBOR lags; the economic direction and
magnitude are preserved, and the BVAR with HAC-robust LP (Section~\ref{sec:lp})
confirms significance regardless. We retain VARX(4,0) as the baseline.

This paper examined how external shocks --- US monetary policy tightening and
China growth slowdowns --- transmit to Hong Kong's real economy under the
currency board. Using a BVAR(4) with Minnesota prior estimated on 112 quarterly
observations, we identified two transmission channels operating at different
speeds.

The central finding is a speed asymmetry within the monetary channel. HIBOR
shocks reach property prices within one quarter, consistent with the collateral
channel of \citet{BernankeGertler1989}, but the property price response is
short-lived. The direct HIBOR--GDP link, by contrast, persists through four
quarters, reflecting credit costs and mortgage rate adjustments that continue
to bind after the asset price response has faded. Property price shocks are the
dominant source of GDP variance (20.9\% at $h = 8$) despite the transient
impulse response, because property collateral amplifies the credit channel for
several subsequent periods. Local projections with HAC-robust inference confirm
all three principal channels: HIBOR--property, HIBOR--GDP, and property--GDP.

[EXPAND: policy implications paragraph]

Several limitations warrant acknowledgement. The CPI equation exhibits a
structural mean break at COVID-19 ($p = 0.029$) that the fixed-coefficient prior
does not fully absorb, and the exports series is nominal. The ZLB asymmetry
result is directionally consistent with impaired transmission at the zero lower
bound but is imprecisely estimated ($n = 36$ ZIRP observations). Future work
with a longer sample could exploit time-varying parameter methods to formally
test whether the transmission architecture evolved across the post-COVID
normalisation.

\title{External Shock Propagation to Hong Kong Under the Currency Board:\\
A VAR Analysis of US Monetary Policy and China Growth Spillovers}
\author{Steven Chung}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
How do US monetary policy shocks and China growth shocks propagate to
Hong Kong's real economy under the currency board? I estimate a
six-variable quarterly VAR for Hong Kong (1998Q1--2023Q3) using data
from Hong Kong's Census and Statistics Department, the HKMA, and FRED.
Three identification strategies are employed: Cholesky ordering,
sign restrictions, and a VECM exploiting four cointegrating
relationships.

The reduced-form VAR finds that China GDP growth explains 41\% of
Hong Kong GDP forecast error variance at the two-year horizon, while
the US federal funds rate explains 32\% of HIBOR variance. The VECM,
which preserves the long-run currency board equilibrium, reveals a
stronger US channel: the FFR explains 62\% of HIBOR and 36\%
of unemployment variance. Sign-restricted IRFs confirm that a
US monetary tightening raises HIBOR, depresses GDP, and increases
unemployment, while a positive China growth shock boosts GDP and CPI.
Time-varying parameter estimates show the China coefficient on
Hong Kong GDP has strengthened since the GFC.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} Hong Kong, currency board, VAR, VECM, FEVD,
sign restrictions, monetary transmission, China spillovers

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{JEL Classification:} E32, E52, F41, F42
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ==================================================================

Hong Kong operates one of the world's most rigid exchange rate regimes:
a currency board pegging the Hong Kong dollar to the US dollar at a
narrow band since 1983. Under this arrangement, Hong Kong imports US
monetary policy directly---the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA)
adjusts the base rate in lockstep with the Federal Reserve. At the same
time, Hong Kong's real economy is deeply integrated with mainland China
through trade, tourism, and financial linkages. This creates a natural
tension: the interest rate channel is anchored to US conditions, while
the real activity channel is driven by Chinese demand.

This paper quantifies how US monetary policy shocks and China growth
shocks propagate to Hong Kong's domestic economy. Using a six-variable
quarterly VAR estimated on 1998Q1--2023Q3 data from official Hong Kong
sources, I employ three identification strategies---Cholesky ordering,
sign restrictions, and a VECM that preserves the long-run currency
board equilibrium.

The main findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The US channel is stronger than Cholesky alone suggests.}
    The reduced-form VAR attributes 32\% of HIBOR variance to the US FFR
    at the 8-quarter horizon. The VECM, which preserves cointegrating
    relationships, raises this to 62\%, revealing that Cholesky
    identification underestimates the currency board passthrough by
    differencing away the long-run peg equilibrium.

    \item \textbf{China dominates real activity, but less than expected.}
    China GDP growth explains 41\% of Hong Kong GDP forecast error
    variance in the VAR and 28\% in the VECM. Own shocks remain large,
    suggesting substantial domestic business cycle dynamics.

    \item \textbf{Sign restrictions confirm the transmission channels.}
    Theory-consistent sign restrictions identify a US monetary tightening
    shock that raises HIBOR and depresses GDP, and a China growth shock
    that boosts GDP and CPI. The median IRFs are consistent with the
    Cholesky results.

    \item \textbf{Time-varying parameters show deepening China linkages.}
    TVP-VAR estimates reveal that the China GDP coefficient on Hong Kong
    GDP has strengthened since the GFC, consistent with growing mainland
    economic integration.
\end{enumerate}

These results have direct policy relevance for HKMA's macroprudential
toolkit: since the interest rate channel is imported and the dominant
real shock source (China) cannot be offset by domestic monetary policy,
fiscal and prudential instruments must bear the stabilization burden.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ==================================================================

This paper contributes to three strands of literature.

\paragraph{Small open economy VARs.}
The structural VAR framework for analyzing external shock transmission
to small open economies originates with \citet{cushman1997identifying}
and \citet{kim2001international}. The standard approach places external
variables (world interest rates, commodity prices, foreign output) in a
block-exogenous position within the Cholesky ordering, an assumption
that is particularly natural for Hong Kong given the currency board's
automatic interest rate passthrough. Recent work by
\citet{uhlig2005effects} and \citet{rubio2010structural} has extended
identification beyond Cholesky to sign restrictions.

\paragraph{China--Hong Kong economic linkages.}
\citet{genberg2009monetary} study monetary transmission under the HKD
peg and find that US monetary conditions dominate short-term interest
rates but have limited direct effects on output. More recently,
\citet{he2014balancing} examine how mainland economic conditions
increasingly shape Hong Kong's business cycle. My contribution is to
provide a unified quantification of both channels using FEVD, sign
restrictions, and VECM, all within a single empirical framework.

\paragraph{Currency board macro dynamics.}
The macroeconomics of currency boards is surveyed by
\citet{hanke2002currency}. Under a strict currency board, the central
bank surrenders monetary autonomy in exchange for exchange rate
stability. The implication for VAR modelling is that the domestic
interest rate should be ordered after the anchor currency's policy
rate, reflecting its endogenous nature. The VECM extension is
particularly relevant here, as the FFR--HIBOR spread constitutes a
natural cointegrating relationship under the peg.

Variable & Description & Unit & Source \\
\midrule
\texttt{us\_ffr} & US effective federal funds rate & \% & FRED (FEDFUNDS) \\
\texttt{china\_gdp} & China nominal GDP growth & YoY \% & FRED (CHNGDPNQDSMEI) \\
\texttt{gdp\_growth} & HK real GDP growth & YoY \% & C\&SD table 310-30001 \\
\texttt{cpi\_inflation} & HK CPI inflation & YoY \% & C\&SD splice + WB fallback \\

Hong Kong GDP growth is obtained from the Census and Statistics
Department's official quarterly figures via their public API.
The unemployment rate is the seasonally adjusted 3-month moving average
from C\&SD. The 3-month HIBOR is sourced directly from the HKMA's
end-of-month fixing data, aggregated to quarterly means. The US federal
funds rate and China GDP are from FRED. CPI inflation uses an official
C\&SD monthly YoY splice where available (currently from 2020Q2 onward),
with World Bank annual interpolation retained only as a fallback for
earlier periods.

\subsection{Stationarity and Cointegration}

I test each variable for stationarity using both ADF and KPSS tests.
Where both tests agree on a unit root, I take first differences.
Four variables are differenced: \texttt{china\_gdp},
\texttt{cpi\_inflation}, \texttt{unemployment}, and \texttt{hibor\_3m}.

Johansen cointegration testing on the I(1) subset detects \emph{four}
cointegrating relationships at the 90\% level (trace statistics:
82.7, 37.9, 18.5, 3.8). This strong cointegration---particularly
the FFR--HIBOR spread under the currency board---motivates the VECM
specification as a primary robustness check.

\section{Econometric Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% ==================================================================

\subsection{VAR Specification}

The reduced-form VAR(\(p\)) is:
\[
y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + \cdots + A_p y_{t-p} + u_t,
\quad u_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_u)
\]
where \(y_t\) is the \(K \times 1\) vector of endogenous variables
(\(K = 6\)), \(c\) is a vector of intercepts, \(A_\ell\) are
\(K \times K\) coefficient matrices, and \(u_t\) are reduced-form
residuals. Lag order \(p = 2\) is selected by BIC subject to a
parameter-count guardrail (19 parameters per equation, ratio 0.19).

\subsection{Cholesky Identification}

I identify structural shocks via a Cholesky decomposition of
\(\Sigma_u = PP'\), where \(P\) is lower-triangular. The variable
ordering follows the external-first convention:
\[
[\text{us\_ffr}, \; \text{china\_gdp}, \; \text{gdp\_growth}, \;
\text{cpi\_inflation}, \; \text{unemployment}, \; \text{hibor\_3m}]
\]
This ordering assumes that US and China variables do not respond
contemporaneously to Hong Kong shocks (small open economy assumption).

\subsection{Sign Restrictions}

Following \citet{uhlig2005effects} and \citet{rubio2010structural},
I also identify shocks via sign restrictions. The structural impact
matrix is \(A_0 = PQ\) where \(Q\) is a random orthogonal rotation.
I draw 5,000 candidate rotations and retain those satisfying:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{US monetary tightening:} FFR $\uparrow$, HIBOR $\uparrow$,
    GDP $\downarrow$, unemployment $\uparrow$ (on impact)
    \item \textbf{China growth shock:} China GDP $\uparrow$, GDP $\uparrow$,
    CPI $\uparrow$ (on impact)
\end{itemize}

The median and 68\% pointwise bands from the accepted draws provide
set-identified IRFs.
Because estimation applies first differences to selected variables,
the sign restrictions are imposed on innovations in the transformed
system; interpretation in levels is therefore local and model-based
rather than a direct level-accounting identity.

\subsection{Vector Error Correction Model}

Given the four cointegrating relationships detected by Johansen, I
estimate a VECM:
\[
\Delta y_t = \alpha \beta' y_{t-1} + \sum_{i=1}^{p-1}
\Gamma_i \Delta y_{t-i} + c + u_t
\]
where \(\beta'\) contains the cointegrating vectors and \(\alpha\)
contains the adjustment coefficients. The first cointegrating vector
loads primarily on \texttt{us\_ffr} and \texttt{hibor\_3m} with
coefficient $-1.16$, confirming the currency board equilibrium:
HIBOR tracks FFR plus a stable spread.

\subsection{Time-Varying Parameters}

To address potential structural breaks (GFC, COVID), I estimate a
TVP-VAR using a Kalman filter with forgetting factor $\lambda = 0.99$
\citep{koop2013large}. The state evolution is:
\[
\theta_t = \theta_{t-1} + \eta_t, \quad Q_t = (\lambda^{-1} - 1) P_{t|t-1}
\]
This allows all VAR coefficients to drift smoothly over time without
requiring break-date specification.

\subsection{FEVD and Historical Decomposition}

FEVD and historical decomposition follow standard formulas:
\[
\text{FEVD}_{i,j}(h) = \frac{\sum_{s=0}^{h} \theta_{ij,s}^2}
{\sum_{s=0}^{h} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \theta_{ik,s}^2}, \qquad
y_t - \bar{y} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} \sum_{s=0}^{t-1} \Theta_s \,
e_{j,t-s}
\]

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ==================================================================

\subsection{Granger Causality}

Pairwise Granger causality tests confirm the expected transmission
channels. China GDP Granger-causes HK GDP growth ($p = 0.020$),
CPI inflation ($p < 0.001$), and unemployment ($p < 0.001$).
The US FFR Granger-causes CPI inflation ($p = 0.006$), unemployment
($p = 0.002$), and HIBOR ($p < 0.001$). The FFR also Granger-causes
GDP growth at the 5\% level ($p = 0.044$), suggesting a measurable
external-rate channel to real activity in this sample.

The central finding is that the importance of the US channel depends
critically on how cointegration is handled:

\begin{itemize}
    \item In the \textbf{reduced-form VAR} (which differences I(1)
    variables), the US FFR explains 32\% of HIBOR variance and 22\%
    of unemployment. China GDP explains 41\% of HK GDP.

    \item In the \textbf{VECM}, which preserves the long-run
    FFR--HIBOR peg equilibrium, the US channel strengthens notably:
    62\% for HIBOR and 36\% for unemployment. The Cholesky VAR
    understates the currency board passthrough because differencing
    removes the long-run equilibrium relationship that the peg
    creates.

    \item This result has a clear economic interpretation: the
    currency board \emph{is} a cointegrating relationship. Ignoring
    it (as in the differenced VAR) attenuates the measured US influence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sign-Restricted IRFs}

Sign restrictions accept 98 of 5,000 draws (2.0\% acceptance rate),
indicating that the four simultaneous sign constraints are informative.
The median IRFs show:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A US monetary tightening raises HIBOR by approximately
    100 basis points on impact, with the effect persisting for
    6--8 quarters. GDP growth declines by 0.3--0.5 percentage
    points, and unemployment rises gradually.
    \item A positive China growth shock raises HK GDP by 0.5--1.0
    percentage points and CPI by 0.2--0.4 points, with effects
    concentrated in the first 4 quarters.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Historical Decomposition}

The historical decomposition identifies which shocks drove specific
episodes. During the 2008--09 Global Financial Crisis, both China
growth and US FFR shocks contribute negatively to Hong Kong's GDP
decline. During 2022--23, the US monetary tightening shock dominates
the rise in HIBOR, while China's post-COVID slowdown drives real
economy weakness.

\subsection{Time-Varying Parameters}

The TVP-VAR reveals that the lag-1 coefficient of China GDP on
HK GDP growth has increased from approximately 0.05 in the early
2000s to 0.15 by 2020, consistent with deepening trade and financial
integration. The US FFR coefficient on HIBOR has remained relatively
stable, reflecting the mechanical nature of the currency board
passthrough.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}
% ==================================================================

\subsection{Cholesky Ordering}

I re-estimate under three orderings: (i) external-first (baseline),
(ii) domestic-first, and (iii) reversed. The qualitative
result---China dominates real activity, US dominates HIBOR---is
preserved across all orderings, though exact FEVD shares shift by
5--15 percentage points.

\subsection{Sub-sample Stability}

I estimate on full sample, pre-GFC, post-GFC, and ex-COVID
sub-samples. The full sample and ex-COVID models are stable
(max eigenvalue $< 1$). The post-GFC sub-sample shows mild
instability, likely reflecting the shorter sample.

\subsection{VAR vs.\ VECM}

The VECM comparison (\Cref{tab:fevd}) provides the most important
robustness insight: accounting for cointegration substantially
amplifies the US monetary channel. This is not a statistical artifact
but an economic implication of the currency board---the long-run
FFR--HIBOR relationship is a structural feature of the monetary regime.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==================================================================

This paper provides a multi-method quantitative answer to how US and
China shocks propagate to Hong Kong under the currency board. Three
key insights emerge.

First, the standard VAR with Cholesky identification underestimates
the US monetary channel because differencing removes the cointegrating
relationship created by the currency board peg. The VECM, which
preserves this equilibrium, reveals that the US FFR explains 62\% of
HIBOR variance and 36\% of unemployment variance.

Second, China GDP growth remains the dominant external driver of
Hong Kong's real economy (28--41\% of GDP variance depending on
specification),
and this influence has strengthened since the GFC as measured by the
TVP-VAR. However, own shocks still dominate GDP variance (56--64\%),
suggesting substantial domestic business cycle dynamics.

Third, sign restrictions corroborate the Cholesky results: a US
monetary tightening raises HIBOR, depresses GDP, and increases
unemployment, while a positive China growth shock boosts GDP and CPI.

These results have direct policy implications. Since the currency board
imports US monetary conditions regardless of Hong Kong's cyclical
position, and the dominant real shock source (China) cannot be offset
by domestic monetary policy, fiscal and macroprudential instruments
must bear the primary stabilization burden.
